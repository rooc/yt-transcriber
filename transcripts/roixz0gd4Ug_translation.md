**0:00** · During the 20th century, the United States had major organized crime bosses. Figures like Al Capone or John Gotti built illegal empires and dominated headlines. But after them, there was no major drug lord in the country. That's why we ask ourselves: if the United States is one of the world's largest drug markets, why doesn't a boss like "El Chapo" or large cartels like in Latin America exist? In this video we explain who really sells drugs inside the United States and why they are hardly talked about.

**0:41** · A few months ago, in a federal court in New York, a scene unfolded that made headlines around the world. Ismael "El Mayo" Zambada — one of the historical leaders of the Sinaloa cartel — pleaded guilty. And he acknowledged that for decades he trafficked enormous quantities of drugs into the United States. What for US authorities meant a historic victory. Pam Bondi, who was US Attorney General, highlighted a message that authorities in this country repeat over and over: the large Latin American cartels are responsible for flooding the United States with drugs.

**1:15** · But this message leaves

**1:54** · out an essential part of the system. The United States is not only the final destination, it is one of the world's largest drug markets. And for that market to function, someone has to handle everything that happens when crossing the border: distributing drugs within the country, selling them on the streets… and handling the money. And this is where, according to some experts, local organizations play a relevant role. But the question is: do "American cartels" really exist?

**2:26** · According to an FBI report, there are more than 30,000 gangs in the United States, with around 1.4 million members. Some of them are the Arizona Mexican Mafia, Barrio Azteca, Border Brothers, Hells Angels, Latin Kings… To name a few. There are those who argue that these groups can be considered cartels. The gringo cartels are similar to those of Colombia, Mexico and Central America.

**2:56** · What happens is that the world… got used to the narrative from the United States, from the federal government, that cartels exist somewhere else but not in their territory. This was true until a few years ago, until the cunning of the Mexican cartels, who have learned the danger of operating in the United States.

**3:23** · They continue to be the main wholesale suppliers of narcotics to the United States, but for decades, US citizens have participated in the sale, transport, distribution and money laundering from narcotics. Although Esquivel believes that DEA officials and other government agencies do not benefit from talking about "American cartels".

**3:48** · If they were to acknowledge them as such… it would not have the political merit to pressure another country like Mexico now under Donald Trump's threat of invading with military forces to combat drug trafficking…. What they don't want to talk about are two very simple things. First, they already have cartels, and second, they neglected a public health and education problem that is now gigantic and very difficult to stop."

**4:19** · However, there are those who do not believe that organizations in the US can be compared to Latin American cartels. First, because of their way of operating. For Steven Dudley, of Insight Crime, gangs in the United

**5:33** · States do not have the power to corrupt authorities as happens in Latin America.

**5:38** · He believes that their connections with law enforcement, as well as with political and economic elites, are minimal and that they barely have the capacity to challenge the state's monopoly on power. The second factor is their structure. There is no "boss of bosses" who controls everything. Instead, they operate as independent cells: each group has its own leaders, its own rules and its own way of operating. Their dominance is much more fragmented. They control neighborhoods, streets… even corners. This makes them harder to dismantle.

**6:08** · If one falls, others keep functioning. Additionally, according to some experts, they have no "loyalty" to any supplier. They work with whoever offers the best conditions. That gives them flexibility and power. But let's go back to the initial question. Why doesn't a major American boss exist? According to experts, criminal leaders do not benefit from having notoriety.

**6:37** · In the United States there is a strong presence of agencies and police corporations, as well as harsh penalties that would easily strip them of their profits. Added to this is that the criminal system structure of the gangs does not need a major boss to function. In fact, avoiding visible figures is an advantage.

**7:22** · The less visibility, the less risk. But beyond considerations, the fact is that, far from stopping, drug consumption in the United States has grown in recent decades. And cartels from Latin America, Asia and other parts of the world remain the main suppliers.
